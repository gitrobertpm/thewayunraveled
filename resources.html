Resources

Here is a great list of linked resources that can be of tremendous help. It's an easy to read list of positive influences and most of what you should know, conveniently all in one place.

Please, don't feel overwhelmed by the material's size or content. You don't have to read it all in one day. That would be ridiculous. Instead, come back from time to time, at your own pace, to give yourself a boost and maybe fill in a few possible gaps in your knowledge base.

THE WEB

These sites are worth a visit and can be very helpful.

  1. A Better You from TED Talks

    2. A video collection of interesting and insightful talks from successful professionals about issues related to you being a better you.

  2. How Stuff works

    3. Here, the title says it all. If you want to know how stuff works, this site can probably help you out.

  3. Science News

    4. A great place to get the latest updates on everything from biology to cosmology. ton's of cool stuff here.

  4. PolitiFact

    5. A Pulitzer Prize winning site that works to expose the truth and bring you non-partisan facts. A great place to find the truth.

  5. FactCheck.org

    6. A politically neutral site that wades through the crap to bring you the truth. Full of political current events, this site uncovers its share of government‐related misrepresentations and outright lies. Another great place to find the truth.

  6. how-to-meditate.org

    7. If you've always wanted to try mediation, but you haven't known where to begin, this can be a great jumping off point. But keep in mind that there are many varieties of mediation, and at the end of the day, your free to do it how ever it best works for you.

GOOD READING

These books are real game changers, and worth a trip to the library.

  1. Leaves of Grass by Walt whitman

    2. Uncle Walt has been called the definitive great American poet. On occasion, deciphering his prose may require a little extra thought and consideration — after all, it was written well over one hundred years ago, and the man had an unparalleled gift for words — but many of his lines are real show‐stoppers that uplift, inspire, and quench a thirst for life.

  2. The Doors of Perception by Aldous Huxely

    3. With brilliantly lucid and descriptive writing about what the book calls the “consciousness‐restricting valve of the mind,” Huxely cracks open the egg of reality and wonderfully pokes at its yoke.

  3. Be Here Now by Ram Dass

    4. This book is somewhat unorthodox in its structure and content, but it lacks not its fair share of perhaps the greatest variety of wisdom.

  4. The World as I See It by Albert Einstein

    5. A.E. was arguably one of the greatest men every to bless this world with an appearance. And not just for his incredible contributions, or even his next level brilliance, but also for his unshakable compassion towards all mankind, his absolute rejection of those things that cause harm, and of course his love of learning and science. This book is just a collection of his letters, writings, and thoughts, and they nicely demonstrate the true goodness that resided inside of one of Earth's favorite children, Albert Einstein.

  5. The Tao of Pooh by Benjamin Hoff

    6. A light fun read that offers interesting insights into the concepts of character, attitude, point of view and the ways in which people live their lives. It does this by drawing a very clever connection between author A.A. Milne's stories about the lovable Winnie‐the‐Pooh character and the wonderful belief system, that is more philosophy than religion, known as Taoism

  6. The Dancing Wu Li Masters by Gary Zukov

    7. This book very neatly highlights the interesting correlation between quantum mechanics and the earthy esoteric philosophies of ancient eastern sages. It gets a little technical at times, but remains firmly in the wheelhouse of the layman, thanks to Zukov's great explanations.

  7. The Politics of Ecstacy by Timothy Leary

    8. Dr. Leary might have been out there, so to speak, but he was only trying to save the world. In this counter‐culture classic, this very educated frontiersman of reality and the mind made a number of strong points and a very convincing argument for the way things really are.

  8. The Art of War by Sun Tzu

    9. It's not always easy to see the ways in which the lessons of this book can be applied to modern daily life, but this ancient collection of wisdom is not merely a manual for conflict, it is a guide to understanding the terrible cost of conflict, and the ultimate importance of avoiding it whenever possible.

PRACTICES

Here are a few things anyone can do to improve the quality of their days.

  1. Make a list

    2. The simple act of creating a list can be the difference between success and a missed opportunity.

  2. Be present in the moment

    3. What a shame it is that though we are always in the now, so often our thoughts are darting elsewhere. Not being present in the moment is maybe something like saving all the fun stuff until you are asleep. Ridiculous.

  3. Stay positive

    4. Point of view just could potentially have a greater effect on the quality of a day than the temperature, the weather, money, and even health.

  4. Be nice

    5. Every act of cruelty only adds to the already untold enormity of the crappiness in the world. Likewise, every act of kindness, compassion, and generosity succeeds in making the world a cooler place to live.

  5. Meditate and reflect

    6. Learning to calm, control, and understand the mind just might be the greatest skill one can master.

  6. Actively improve life

    7. Going nowhere and doing nothing gets you exactly that… a big fat nothing. In life, as in most things, the more you put in, the more you tend to get in return.

Without learning, we wouldn't have advanced medicine, awesome modern conveniences, soap, or even running water.

Gandhi said, "Live as if you were to die tomorrow. Learn as if you were to live for every."

Never stop learning. ☯
